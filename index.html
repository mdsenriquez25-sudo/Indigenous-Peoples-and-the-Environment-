Indigenous Peoples And The Environment

ENVIRONMENTAL CONSERVATION AND SUSTAINABILITY PRACTICES OF INDIGENOUS PEOPLES

Indigenous peoples manage a large part of the world’s land, but climate change still threatens their livelihoods. By 2100, Philip Alston warns that climate change could create “climate apartheid,” where the rich escape the worst effects while the poor face hunger, conflict, or migration.

Who are we talking about when we refer to indigenous peoples; and what do we mean when we say that land is traditionally owned, managed, or occupied?

There is no single definition of indigenous peoples because they are diverse. According to the United Nations Permanent Forum on Indigenous Issues, they are communities with historical connections to their lands before colonization, strong ties to natural resources, and distinct cultures, languages, and social systems. They maintain traditional knowledge based on long interaction with nature, viewing it as sacred and using this knowledge to protect environmental balance and sustain resources like water, food, soil, shelter, and medicine.

How are indigenous people affected by changes in climate, biodiversity, and ecosystems?

Indigenous peoples are highly affected by environmental degradation and climate change because their lives depend on their land and natural resources. Activities like industrial projects, deforestation, and even some climate solutions such as biofuel plantations can harm their territories. However, when their land and resource rights are protected, both their communities and the environment can flourish.

What role do indigenous people play in developing, managing, and protecting natural space and ecosystem?

Indigenous peoples play an important role in creating solutions for protecting ecosystems. Their traditional knowledge helps in environmental management, sustainable food production, and climate change mitigation. Organizations like United Nations Environment Programme work with the United Nations Permanent Forum on Indigenous Issues to promote traditional knowledge and support ecosystem restoration and sustainability.

On a policy level, how can we ensure that indigenous people are included in the decision- making and management of ecosystem?

The United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples requires that indigenous peoples give free, prior, and informed consent for decisions affecting their rights and lands. Groups like the United Nations Permanent Forum on Indigenous Issues and the Expert Mechanism on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples help represent their concerns. Meanwhile, the United Nations Environment Programme supports the protection of environmental defenders and works with partners such as the Interfaith Rainforest Initiative to protect indigenous rights, traditional knowledge, and nature.

Causes of Biodiversity Loss in Indigenous Lands in the Philippines

• Logging. Long-term logging has damaged forests by reducing species diversity and disrupting natural energy, water, and nutrient cycles.

• Small-scale upland agriculture. Slash-and-burn farming was once sustainable with crop rotation, but it became unsustainable as increasing numbers of landless migrants overused the land.

• Over-hunting. Unregulated hunting for food, training, or recreation harms biodiversity and disrupts ecosystem balance by preventing wildlife from fulfilling their natural roles.

• Illegal fishing. Destructive fishing methods and overfishing cause biodiversity loss in aquatic ecosystems by damaging habitats, killing marine life, and reducing the productivity of rivers and fishing grounds.

• Forest fire. During extreme heat temperature, our forest can reach its kindling temperature and a fire starts.

• Improper grazing. Ruminants placed in a pasture land where they just freely roam around in open farmland can cause damage or destruction to other species of the flora and fauna.

• Construction and conversion of forest areas into residential lots. The construction and conversion of forest areas into residential lots has threatened indigenous species with the loss of their natural habitat and breeding environment.

Issues and Problems on Biodiversity Conservation of Indigenous Lands in the Philippines

• Overexploitation. Overexploitation of preferred tree species for construction, handicrafts, and other uses, driven by growing community demand, leads to a decline in their population.

• Land use alteration. Clearing forests for agriculture, especially through slash-and-burn practices before the rainy season, is a serious problem in some Indigenous communities.

• Forest fire. Forest fires, often unintentionally caused by burning agricultural clearings, harm most plant and animal species and commonly occur in grasslands or areas under natural succession during summer or prolonged drought.

•Limited knowledge of the global value of biodiversity. Many provincial community members have limited awareness of biodiversity’s broader environmental benefits—such as environmental protection, carbon sequestration, and climate change mitigation—despite some limited information dissemination efforts.

• Limited capacity of barangay government units. Though national environmental laws and even local government ordinances for biodiversity protection are existing, the barangay government units cannot implement such laws due to their limited financial, physical, and manpower resources

Lack of sustainable livelihood and alternative sources of income. Due to the absence of sustainable livelihood and a source of income, people tend to look to the available natural resources as a source of their subsistence and income.

Contemporary Challenges on Land Use and Management in Philippine Indigenous Lands

• Issues on the ancestral domain; loss of land property. Although ancestral domains were generally easy to identify and delineate based on the locations of ethnic communities, boundary issues and disputes still exist between some provinces.

• Land titling. This is the act of formal government to classify lands according to their different uses as the watershed, forest or timberland, parks and wildlife reservations, alienable and disposable lands for agriculture, residential and commercial use.

• Threat to the watershed. Although dams were built to irrigate fields, generate electricity, and provide income for provinces, there is a need to review profit-sharing schemes to ensure fair distribution and better protection of the watershed ecosystem.

• Boundary disputes. Ongoing boundary disputes between Indigenous communities, particularly in neighboring provinces, need to be resolved promptly as they hinder progress.
